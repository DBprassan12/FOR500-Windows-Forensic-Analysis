1.3 Core Windows Forensics: Focus on Analysis

Understanding OS and Applications

A forensic examiner/analyst should have a solid understanding of the operating system and the application he is examining.

this will help you understand where to look for evidence of an action.

Many times, you will find that you will have to conduct tests in your lab on applications to find what evidence is created by the application when an action is taken, and then go to the hard drive you are analyzing and look for that piece of evidence.

It is important to determine the scope of the search.

Understanding Evidence Created

Evidence of action or activity is created by both the user of the computer and the system itself.

Evidence created by a system action might be an audit log showing default system maintenance has run at the default time or file access times being changed by an antivirus scan automatically being run.

Requires Analysis

Analysis is the act of looking at all the individual findings, including the existence of data, or lack thereof, as well as associated metadata (location of the file, timestamps), and determining which actions were taken on the computer that would have caused those artifacts to exist.

Using this, we can determine the who, what, when, where, why, and how.

The proper way to accomplish this is not simply to recover a bunch of artifacts and recover some deleted items, paste them into a report, and hand them to a prosecutor or management.

You need to recover the artifacts and then analyze the data that they hold to determine a clear picture of what the user was doing, when they were doing it, why, and many other details that might go unnoticed unless your trained eye began to examine them.

your expert opinion is based on conducting an analysis of the totality of your findings, and the information you have about the investigation as to what events took place and by whom.

Your opinion must be in turn supported by your findings.

Setting Goals and preparation

One of the most common mistakes digital investigative analysts make when they receive digital evidence is to immediately go into hunt mode and start poking around looking for evidence.

Its important to prepare by developing a investigative roadmap and set goal. Each analysis has a goal.

Spending a few minutes at the beginning of a digital investigative analysis preparing a digital investigative plan (DIP) can help focus your efforts.

Goal types

Binary:

Those goals can be binary, like "Did Fred Rocba steal intellectual property?" How many secret or confidential documents do you need to find to satisfy this goal? The answer is ONE.

Generally quantitative:

This means that the progress of your goal has to be determined in an objective approach.

Fully quantitative:

Some cases require identification of every piece of evidence. In these fully quantitative situations, the analyst must sift through the evidence with a fine-tooth comb to find every classified document taken so a full assessment can be made of the damage to the company

Digital investigative plan (DIP)

  • The first mandatory area of a typical DIP is a basic background of the investigation to orient and give the analyst context.
  • The second mandatory item in a DIP is a clear, detailed explanation of what is being requested. An understanding of the scope of the investigation is necessary
  • The third mandatory item in a DIP is a Plan of action.
